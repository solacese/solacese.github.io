Solace SDR Playbook — Competitive & Objections

“We just use our cloud provider’s messaging”

AWS SQS/SNS, Azure Service Bus, Google Pub/Sub — they think native is enough. It probably is, for now. Your job is to make them curious about what happens when “for now” runs out.

⚡

Core principle: Cloud-native messaging services are good at what they do. Don’t trash them. The opening is that they’re designed for one cloud — and the moment a company needs to connect across clouds, regions, on-prem, or edge, the native service becomes a wall, not a bridge. You’re selling the question, not the answer.

1
“We use SQS / Service Bus / Pub/Sub”
Validate, Then Open the Door
▾
They’re telling you they already have messaging. They probably chose it because it was there — zero procurement, already in their cloud console. That’s a rational decision. Don’t fight it.
Say this

“Makes sense — those services are solid for what they do. A lot of our customers started the same way. Where it usually gets interesting is when they need to connect beyond a single cloud — another region, on-prem systems, a partner, or a second cloud provider. That’s when native messaging starts to feel limiting. Is that something you’re running into, or likely to?”

Why it works

Validates their choice. “A lot of our customers started the same way” normalises the journey without implying they’re behind. The question at the end is genuine — if the answer is “no, we’re single cloud forever” then there may not be a fit, and that’s fine. But most enterprises aren’t single cloud forever.

Don’t do this

Don’t say “SQS isn’t enterprise-grade” or “Service Bus loses messages.” Even if there are real limitations, the person you’re talking to chose it or approved it. Don’t make them defend that decision.

2
They push — “We’re all-in on Azure / AWS / GCP”
The Multi-Cloud Reality Check
▾
They believe they’re single-cloud. They might be right today. But very few large enterprises stay that way. And even “single cloud” often means multiple regions, on-prem SAP, factory systems, or partner connections.
Option A — The hidden complexity angle
Say this

“That makes sense. Quick question though — is everything in one cloud? Or do you have on-prem systems, SAP, factories, or partners that also need real-time data? Because what we see is that even ‘all-in’ companies end up with hybrid connections they didn’t plan for. That’s where a layer above the cloud-native service starts to pay for itself.”

Option B — The lock-in angle (use carefully)
Say this

“We hear that a lot. The question our customers usually wrestle with is: what happens if the cloud strategy changes? An acquisition, a new regulation, a partnership that runs on a different cloud. If your messaging is native to one provider, migrating that layer is a massive project. Solace sits above the cloud — so if the strategy shifts, the messaging doesn’t have to.”

Option C — The real proof point
Say this

“One of our customers — a global beauty brand — started on Google Pub/Sub. It worked fine until they hit production volumes and started losing messages. They moved to Solace and the problem disappeared. I’m not saying that’s your situation, but it’s worth knowing before it happens to you.”

Why it works

Option A gently exposes the hybrid reality most enterprises face. Option B plants a strategic seed about future flexibility without being alarmist. Option C uses a real customer proof point (Groupe Rocher / Google Pub/Sub) — concrete and memorable. All three avoid positioning cloud-native as bad — they position Solace as the layer above.

Don’t do this

Don’t get into a feature comparison between SQS and Solace. Don’t use the phrase “vendor lock-in” — it sounds like a sales pitch. Say “flexibility” instead. Don’t assume they have multi-cloud problems — ask.

3
Close or release
Book It or Respect the No
▾
If you’ve opened the multi-cloud or hybrid question, they’re either seeing the relevance or they’re genuinely single-cloud-and-happy. Both are fine.
If warm — “hmm, we are looking at multi-cloud / hybrid”
Say this

“That’s exactly when this conversation pays off. Let me get you 20 minutes with someone who can show you how other companies in [their industry] handled that transition — and what they wish they’d known earlier. What does your calendar look like?”

If they acknowledge SAP or on-prem connections
Say this

“So you’ve already got a hybrid situation — cloud plus on-prem SAP. That’s exactly where Solace fits. SAP actually embeds our technology for their event-driven architecture. It might be worth seeing how that connects to your Azure / AWS setup. 20 minutes?”

If firm — “we’re good with what we have”
Say this

“Totally fair. If the cloud strategy evolves or you start connecting beyond a single provider, we’re an easy conversation. Appreciate your time.”

Why it works

The SAP angle is particularly powerful here — almost every enterprise has SAP, and the fact that SAP embeds Solace (as SAP AEM) is a credibility proof point that changes the conversation from “why would I add another tool” to “oh, this is already in my SAP world.” The firm exit plants a seed tied to a real future event (strategy change), not a vague “maybe someday.”

Quick Rules for the Cloud-Native Conversation

✓ Always

Validate their cloud choice. They picked it for good reasons — zero friction, familiar console, no procurement.

✓ Always

Position Solace as “the layer above.” Not a replacement for SQS/Service Bus — the thing that connects them to everything else.

✓ Always

Ask about hybrid realities: SAP, on-prem, factories, partners, second cloud. Most enterprises have them even if they say “all-in.”

✓ Always

Use the SAP angle when relevant. SAP embeds Solace as AEM — it’s a powerful credibility play.

✗ Never

Say “vendor lock-in.” It sounds like FUD. Say “flexibility” or “portability” instead.

✗ Never

Compare features with SQS, SNS, Service Bus, or Pub/Sub. Your SE can do that in the meeting.

✗ Never

Assume they have a multi-cloud problem. Ask first. If they’re genuinely single-cloud and happy, respect it.

✗ Never

Trash cloud-native services. The person on the call might be the one who architected the current solution.
