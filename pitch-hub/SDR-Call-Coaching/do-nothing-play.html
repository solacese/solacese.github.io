Solace SDR Playbook — Competitive & Objections

“We’re not looking at anything right now”

The hardest objection isn’t a competitor — it’s inertia. They’re not saying no to Solace. They’re saying no to change. Your job is to make the cost of staying still feel worth a conversation.

⚡

Core principle: You can’t create urgency they don’t feel. But you can plant a question they can’t un-hear. The best “do nothing” plays don’t push — they leave the prospect wondering what they might be missing. Curiosity is stronger than pressure.

1
“We’re not looking right now”
Acknowledge, Then Shift the Frame
▾
This isn’t a rejection of Solace — it’s a rejection of disruption. They’re busy. Respect that, then make staying still feel like an active choice rather than a default.
Option A — The peer angle
Say this

“That’s completely fair. Most of the companies we work with weren’t looking either — until they saw what their competitors or peers were doing with real-time data. We’re not asking you to change anything today. But the companies that take 20 minutes to understand what’s possible tend to move faster when the time comes.”

Option B — The strategic timing angle
Say this

“Understood. Quick question though — are you running any SAP modernisation, cloud migration, or integration consolidation projects in the next 12 months? Because the companies that get the most from what we do tend to build it in at the start of those projects, not bolt it on after.”

Why it works

Option A uses peer pressure gently — “competitors or peers” triggers loss aversion without being aggressive. Option B connects to projects they likely do have in motion, reframing Solace as relevant to something already on their roadmap. Both avoid pushing against inertia directly.

Don’t do this

Don’t challenge their assessment. Don’t say “but you should be looking.” Don’t imply they’re behind. They’ll dig in harder. Inertia responds to curiosity, not pressure.

2
They’re still lukewarm
Drop a Proof Point, Not a Pitch
▾
They haven’t hung up, but they’re not leaning in either. One concrete proof point — matched to their world — is worth more than ten minutes of positioning.
If they’re in retail / CPG / manufacturing
Say this

“Just to give you a sense — one of the world’s largest [food producers / brewers / retailers] was in a similar spot. They weren’t looking either. Then they hit a peak period and their integration platform couldn’t keep up — lost orders, duplicate data. They added Solace as a layer and the problem disappeared. I’m not saying that’s you, but it’s the kind of thing that’s worth knowing about before it happens.”

If they’re in financial services / capital markets
Say this

“For context — some of the largest exchanges and banks in the world run on Solace for their most latency-sensitive operations. Not because they were looking to replace something, but because they hit a ceiling with what they had. That’s usually how the conversation starts.”

If the industry is unclear
Say this

“The pattern we see is pretty consistent — companies aren’t looking until they are. An SAP migration starts, or traffic doubles, or they need to connect a new cloud service, and suddenly the integration layer becomes the bottleneck. We help people get ahead of that moment rather than react to it.”

Why it works

A single, specific proof point anchors the conversation in reality. “I’m not saying that’s you” removes pressure while letting the story land. Industry-matched examples feel relevant, not generic. The general fallback uses a pattern (“companies aren’t looking until they are”) that most leaders have experienced firsthand.

Don’t do this

Don’t stack multiple proof points. One is powerful, three is a pitch. Don’t name the customer unless it’s a public case study. Don’t try to diagnose their environment. You’re planting a seed, not running a discovery.

3
Close or plant the seed
Book It, Defer It, or Leave Gracefully
▾
“Do nothing” objections have three outcomes: they agree to a meeting now, they agree to one later, or they pass. All three are acceptable. The worst outcome is burning the relationship.
If warm — “okay, maybe worth a look”
Say this

“Great. Let me set up 20 minutes with someone from our team who can show you what this looks like for [their industry] — no commitment, just context. What does next week look like?”

If “not now but maybe later”
Say this

“Totally fair. How about this — can I check back in [3 months / next quarter / after your SAP go-live]? That way if the timing’s better, we’re not starting from scratch.”

If firm no
Say this

“No problem at all. If anything changes, we’re easy to find. Appreciate you giving me the time.”

Why it works

The warm close is standard. The deferred close is the secret weapon for “do nothing” — it gets a specific callback date on the books, which is far more valuable than a vague “maybe someday.” Tying the callback to their timeline (a project, a quarter boundary) makes it feel like their idea. The firm no is graceful and preserves the relationship for future outreach.

Don’t do this

Don’t push past a clear no. Don’t leave the deferred option open-ended (“I’ll follow up sometime”). Get a specific date or event to tie the callback to. Don’t send a 10-page follow-up email. If they said no, a brief “good to meet you” note is enough.

Quick Rules for the “Do Nothing” Conversation

✓ Always

Respect the “no.” Inertia is rational. They have priorities and you’re not one of them yet.

✓ Always

Connect to something on their roadmap. SAP, cloud, integration, digital transformation — find their existing motion.

✓ Always

Use one proof point, not a pitch. A single relevant story beats ten minutes of features.

✓ Always

Aim for a deferred meeting if you can’t get a meeting now. A specific callback date is a win.

✗ Never

Create fake urgency. They’ll see through it and trust you less.

✗ Never

Imply they’re behind their peers. Shame doesn’t sell meetings. Curiosity does.

✗ Never

Push past a clear no. The relationship is worth more than one meeting.

✗ Never

Leave a deferred follow-up open-ended. “Let me check back in Q3” beats “I’ll circle back sometime.”
