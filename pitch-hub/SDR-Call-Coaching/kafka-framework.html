Solace SDR Playbook — Competitive & Objections

“How do you compare to Kafka?”

A 3-step call framework for handling the most common objection. Your job is to sell the meeting, not Solace. Every answer should keep curiosity alive.

⚡

Core principle: We don’t have a problem to solve — we have something worth seeing. We sell aspiration and curiosity, not pain. The prospect didn’t call us. We called them. Respect that.

1
The Kafka question lands
The One-Liner
▾
They’ve asked “So how do you compare to Kafka?” — this is a screening question. They’re deciding whether to stay on the call.
Say this

“A lot of our customers use both Solace and Kafka — often together. They’re both great for real-time. Where it gets interesting is that our biggest customers use Solace for the things Kafka wasn’t designed to do. That’s usually what’s worth a conversation.”

Why it works

Normalises Kafka — no threat. “Biggest customers” = social proof + aspiration. “Wasn’t designed to do” = curiosity gap without naming a problem. Ends by framing the meeting as the natural next step.

Don’t do this

Don’t list features. Don’t compare architectures. Don’t say anything negative about Kafka. You’re not here to win a technical debate.

2
They push back — “Like what?”
Keep the Curiosity Gap Open
▾
They’re interested enough to ask a follow-up. This is a good sign. But if you give a specific use case, you hand them something to dismiss. Stay in the curiosity zone.
Option A — Deflect to variety
Say this

“It’s different for every customer, honestly. A global bank uses us for something completely different than an airline or a retailer. That’s actually what makes it a good conversation — it’s less about a feature list and more about what becomes possible in your world. That’s what we’d dig into in 20 minutes.”

Option B — Flip with respect
Say this

“That’s the thing — I don’t want to guess. What I know is the companies getting the most from Solace are doing ambitious things with event-driven architecture. If that’s the direction you’re heading, it’s usually worth a look. If it’s not, I’ll save you the time.”

Why it works

Neither option gives them a specific claim to reject. Option A uses social proof across industries. Option B gives them a dignified exit — which counterintuitively makes them more likely to stay. Both make the meeting the answer, not you.

Don’t do this

Don’t say “operational real-time use cases” or any specific capability. They’ll say “we’ve got that covered” and the call is over. Don’t diagnose their environment. You don’t know it yet and they didn’t ask for help.

3
Close or release
Book It or Respect the No
▾
At this point they’re either leaning in or looking for the exit. Read the signal and move accordingly.
If they’re warm — “yeah, maybe…”
Say this

“Great — I’m not the right person to go deep on this. Let me get you 20 minutes with someone from our team who can show you what this actually looks like for companies in [their industry]. What does your week look like?”

If they’re cold — “not really for us”
Say this

“Totally fair. If things change down the line, we’re easy to find. Appreciate your time.”

Why it works

The warm close acknowledges the SDR’s role honestly — “I’m not the right person to go deep” builds trust and removes pressure. The cold exit is graceful and leaves the door open. People remember how you left the conversation.

Quick Rules for the Kafka Conversation

✓ Always

Normalise Kafka. “Our customers use both.” Never position Solace as a replacement.

✓ Always

Sell the meeting, not Solace. The meeting is where the magic happens.

✓ Always

Use aspirational language. “What becomes possible.” “Ambitious.” “Unlocked.”

✓ Always

Give them a dignified exit. It builds trust and often keeps them on the call.

✗ Never

Name a specific use case or capability. It gives them something to dismiss.

✗ Never

Imply they have a problem. They didn’t call you. You called them.

✗ Never

Get into a technical comparison. You’ll lose to their existing knowledge of Kafka.

✗ Never

Say anything negative about Kafka. It makes you look competitive, not credible.
